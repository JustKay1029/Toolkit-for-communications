Email Templates
Copy, personalise, and send — takes under 2 minutes
Template 1 — Job / Internship Application
Subject: Application for Software Intern Role — [Your Name]

Dear [Hiring Manager's Name],

I am a [year] engineering student at [college] and I am writing to express my interest in the internship role at your organisation. I have attached my resume for your consideration.

I would love the opportunity to discuss how I can contribute to your team. Thank you for your time.

Warm regards,
[Your Name]
Template 2 — Email to a Professor
Subject: Query Regarding [Subject / Project Name]

Dear Professor [Name],

I am [your name] from [batch/section]. I wanted to seek your guidance regarding [topic]. Could we schedule a brief meeting at your convenience?

Thank you for your time.

Regards,
[Your Name]
Template 3 — Follow-up Email
Subject: Following Up — [Previous Topic]

Dear [Name],

I wanted to follow up on my previous email regarding [topic]. Please let me know if you need any additional information from my end.

I look forward to hearing from you.

Regards,
[Your Name]
Do's & Don'ts
✅ Do's
  • Use a clear, specific subject line
  • Keep it under 150 words
  • Always greet and sign off professionally
  • Proofread before sending
❌ Don'ts
  • Use casual language (hey, gonna, wanna)
  • Skip the subject line
  • Send without re-reading once
  • Use all caps or excessive punctuation
Presentation Skills
Deliver with confidence — every time
01
Start with a hook
Open with a surprising fact, a question, or a short story. Grab attention in the first 15 seconds.
02
One idea per slide
Never crowd a slide. One clear idea, supported by 2–3 bullet points max.
03
Eye contact is key
Look at your audience, not the screen. Scan the room — don't fixate on one person.
04
Pause for effect
Silence is powerful. Slow down, pause after key points, and let the audience absorb information.
05
End with a takeaway
Summarise in one sentence. Tell the audience exactly what you want them to remember or do next.
Slide Design Tips
Less is always more
Design Rules
  • Max 6 words per bullet point
  • 2 fonts only — heading + body
  • 2–3 colors throughout the deck
  • Use images over long paragraphs
  • Leave generous white space
Common Mistakes
  • Reading directly off the slide
  • Too many fonts and colors
  • Walls of text on one slide
  • Low contrast text on busy background
  • No clear visual hierarchy

Meeting Communication
Make every meeting count
5 Etiquette Rules
⏰
Join 2 minutes early
Punctuality shows professionalism. Being late disrupts the flow for everyone.
🔇
Mute when not speaking
Background noise is distracting. Keep yourself muted unless you're actively talking.
📷
Keep your camera on
It shows engagement and builds trust. You're part of the meeting — be present.
📝
Take notes + summarise action points
At the end of the meeting, quickly recap: what was decided and who does what by when.
🙋
Ask questions clearly
"Can you clarify what you mean by X?" — specific questions get useful answers.
Before speaking in any meeting, ask yourself three things: Is it relevant? Is it the right time? Is it concise?
Resume Writing Guide
Structure that gets noticed by recruiters
Resume Structure — in this exact order
1
Name + Contact Info + LinkedIn URL
Keep it at the very top. Include phone, email, LinkedIn, and GitHub if relevant.
2
Objective (2 lines max)
One sentence on who you are, one on what you're looking for. Be specific.
3
Education
Degree, college, year of graduation, and CGPA (if above 7.5).
4
Skills — Technical + Soft
List languages, tools, frameworks. Add soft skills like leadership, communication.
5
Projects / Internships
Use action verbs: built, designed, led, improved. Quantify: "Improved speed by 30%".
6
Certifications / Achievements
Coursera, NPTEL, hackathons, competitions — anything that adds credibility.
Key Tips
Keep it to 1 page
Recruiters spend 6–7 seconds on a resume. One tight page beats two loose ones.
Use action verbs
Built, designed, led, improved, developed, managed, optimised, created.
Quantify achievements
"Improved app speed by 30%" beats "worked on app performance" every time.
Tailor for every job
Don't send one generic resume. Adjust skills and objective for each application.
LinkedIn Profile Guide
Build a profile that gets noticed
Section What to Write
Headline Your role + top skills. E.g. "Engineering Student | Python | ML Enthusiast"
About 3–4 lines: who you are, what you do, and what you're looking for. Write in first person.
Experience Internships, part-time roles, major projects with 2–3 bullet points each.
Skills Add at least 10 relevant skills. Ask peers to endorse the top ones.
Photo Professional, clear background, smiling. No selfies or group photos.
Education Add your college, degree, and any relevant coursework or activities.
💡 Connect with 5 new people in your field every week. A message as simple as "I came across your profile and found your work in [field] really interesting" goes a long way.
